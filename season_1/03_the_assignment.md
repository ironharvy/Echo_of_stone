Professor Burov was the kind of man who enjoyed assigning impossible things and then watching to see which students became interesting under pressure. He taught the introductory course on machine intelligence in a lecture hall with tiered wooden desks that had been carved on by three decades of students and a blackboard so old it had gone grey at the edges, as if it were slowly forgetting what it was for. He began the first lecture by writing, on the board, in a hand that did not negotiate:

*BY FRIDAY: A PROGRAM THAT PASSES A CONVERSATION TEST WITH A HUMAN JUDGE FOR FIVE MINUTES. ANY METHOD.*

He underlined *any method* twice.

"You have no budget," he said. "You have the university network. You have each other. You have Friday. The judge will be a colleague of mine who has agreed, with some reluctance, to be bored by you."

A hand went up. "Professor, for a five-minute Turing test we'd need—"

"I did not say Turing test. I said conversation test. Read the board."

The hand stayed up. "Professor, the computational resources—"

"I did not mention resources. Read the board."

The hand came down.

Dmitri, two rows behind Alexei, leaned forward and said into his ear, at a volume that was not a whisper: "He's letting us cheat."

"He's not."

"He's absolutely letting us cheat. He just wrote it on the board. He wrote *any method*. He underlined it twice. If a professor underlines twice, he is begging."

"That's not—"

"Watch."

By Wednesday half the cohort had committed to building something real and were already losing. They were trying to train models they could not train, on hardware they did not have, in languages they had learned the week before. They slept in the computer lab — a long basement room where the monitors cast a greenish light and the ventilation had been set to a temperature that was either ambitious or broken. Coffee cups accumulated on the desks like a geological record. They developed opinions about each other's body odor.

Alexei did not sleep in the computer lab. Alexei reread the assignment on Tuesday evening, alone in Room 314 — Dmitri was out, the porcelain cosmonaut was keeping its counsel, and from upstairs the guitar student had moved on to a second chord that bore no relation to the first. He sat with his folder squared on the desk and thought about what, specifically, Burov had asked for.

*A program that passes a conversation test with a human judge.*

The word *program* did a great deal of work in that sentence. It did not specify where the program ran. It did not specify what the program did. It did not say the program had to understand what it was saying. It did not, he noted, say the program had to generate its responses.

He wrote in the margin of his notebook:

*The program has to pass. The program does not have to think.*

On Friday morning Alexei submitted a short script. The script opened a chat window with the judge and, on receipt of each message, forwarded the message — by way of a trivial relay through two university terminals — to a second chat window, in a different room, which was being typed into by Dmitri.

Dmitri, at the far end of the relay, read the messages and typed replies. Dmitri was very good at sounding like a program trying to sound like a person.

The judge was bored for the full five minutes, exactly as Burov had promised.

Burov sat in on the demonstration. He watched the script run. He watched the messages flow. He watched, specifically, a small indicator in the corner of the relay window that Alexei had not thought to hide, which showed the path of the packet and included, for anyone who looked closely, the room number of the terminal Dmitri was typing from.

At the end of the five minutes the judge announced that he had been conversing with a program, said it had been adequate, and left to get coffee.

Burov did not say anything for a moment. Then:

"Mr Azimov."

"Yes, Professor."

"You have solved the assignment as written."

"Yes, Professor."

"You have not solved the assignment as intended."

"No, Professor."

"You understand these are different things."

"Yes, Professor."

Burov looked at the ceiling. He looked at Alexei. He looked, finally, at Dmitri, who had by then arrived back at the lecture hall at a jog and was standing in the doorway with an expression of a man trying very hard not to look like the second half of a sentence.

"Pass," Burov said. "Both of you. With a note."

"What note, Professor," said Alexei.

"That you have correctly identified that the rules of an institution are the rules it enforces, not the rules it writes. This is useful. It is also dangerous."

"Yes, Professor."

"It will save you, for a while. Then it will cost you."

Alexei did not have a reply for that. Burov did not appear to expect one. He turned, already half-distracted by his next thought, and went out.

Outside the lecture hall Dmitri punched Alexei, once, on the arm — gently, for Dmitri.

"Told you."

"You did."

"I was the cheating. I'd like that on the record."

"It's on the record."

They walked back toward the dorm in a light cold rain that had turned the concrete paths dark and put a shine on the leaves of the uniformly aged trees. Dmitri had his collar up. Alexei did not. He had not noticed it was raining until they were already in it.

Somewhere on the network, in a log nobody would ever look at, the route the judge's packets had taken that morning was preserved, routed through two student terminals on a subnet that should not, strictly speaking, have been able to talk to each other. The subnet connection had been set up by a different student three years earlier and never taken down.

Alexei did not think about that. There was no reason, yet, to think about it.
