The gym still smelled like floor polish and sweat two hours into the dance. Someone had strung red paper lanterns along the bleachers and half of them had slumped; the tape was giving up on heat. A fog machine near the stage coughed out thin ribbons that never quite became atmosphere — they clung ankle-high, drifting between shoes. The DJ kept playing songs two years older than anyone wanted to hear and then apologizing for it between tracks as if apology were also a track.

Alexei stood by the radiator. He had been standing there long enough that the radiator had given up trying to be hot under his shoulder. He could feel the shape of its ridges through his shirt. His jacket was folded over one arm because he had not found anyone to stand with long enough to put it down. Across the floor, people moved in clusters that opened and closed like hands — easy, unselfconscious, part of some choreography he had never been taught.

He had rehearsed it. Walking over, saying her name, asking if she wanted to go outside for a minute. He had rehearsed tone, pace, what to do with his hands. He had, at some point during the sixth chorus of a song he hated, even rehearsed the part where she said yes.

She was laughing near the drinks table. Somebody had said something clever. Alexei didn't know who. He had known her for seven years and he still could not reliably tell when she was actually amused or only being polite. The light caught the thin silver bracelet at her wrist, the one with the single blue bead — he had known the bracelet longer than he had known his own handwriting.

He walked over.

"Julia."

She turned. Her smile didn't fall, exactly. It adjusted. Became the smile for someone she had not expected.

"Alexei. Hi."

He opened his mouth. The rehearsed sentence did not come. A shorter, worse one did.

"Can I talk to you?"

"Sure. Of course."

They moved away from the table, not outside, only to the side wall where a fire-exit sign buzzed faintly. The buzz was on a frequency that got inside teeth. Somewhere behind them the bass dropped and a few people cheered — muffled, like sound heard through water. She waited — still patient, still polite — one hand flat against the painted cinderblock.

He said the thing. He said it badly. He had practiced a version with an opening joke and a version without and in the moment he did neither; he simply said the middle of it, the part that was supposed to come after softening.

She listened all the way through. She did not interrupt, which was worse than interrupting.

When he finished, she looked at him for what felt like a long time. It was probably two seconds.

"Alexei," she said. Quiet. Not unkind. "I always thought you weren't interested."

He heard it land somewhere low in his chest, a small weight set down on a shelf he had not realized was empty.

"I was," he said.

"I know. Now."

Somebody shouted from the dance floor. The song changed — from the old one to another old one. She touched his arm, once, briefly. A gesture so decent it hurt worse than refusal.

"I'm sorry," she said.

He told her it was fine. He told her it was okay. He said a few other words that were not really words, only sounds shaped like being gracious. He could hear himself producing them from somewhere a step behind his own mouth.

She went back to the drinks table. The boy she had been laughing with was waiting with a plastic cup in each hand. He had, Alexei noticed, a very easy face.

Alexei walked outside. The night was cold in the way early spring nights pretend not to be — the air still carrying a memory of afternoon warmth that had already become a lie. He sat on the low stone wall by the parking lot and watched his breath unravel. The stone was rough under his palms, flecked with mica that caught the light from the gym windows. From inside, the music was reduced to its skeleton — just bass and the faint shape of a melody, as if the building were humming to itself.

He was not crying. He was not anything. For a moment he could not feel the stone under him and then he could feel it too much.

A couple came out of the building half-running, half-laughing, holding hands as if holding hands were still a small unbelievable fact. They did not see him. They crossed the lot and were gone. The sound of their laughter persisted longer than they did, caught between the buildings.

He sat a while. A while was all he could measure.

He thought — not in sentences, not yet — that he had been waiting for a cue Julia had never been given. That he had expected her to notice something he had taken care to hide. That he had been quiet for so long that he had made himself into a room no one had needed to enter.

Later, walking home past the shuttered storefronts of a street he had walked a thousand times, he kept his hands in his jacket pockets and his eyes on the pavement. A streetlamp buzzed above a closed pharmacy, its light the same orange as the paper lanterns that were probably still falling in the gym. A cat sat perfectly still on a window ledge across the street, watching him with the calm disinterest of something that had never needed to be noticed.

He passed a closed gallery window. A poster in it showed a white marble figure — a woman, headless, armless, entirely indifferent to being looked at. The floodlight above the glass flickered once and held.

He paused, without meaning to. The figure did not require anything of him. It was beautiful and it was silent and there was nothing it could refuse.

He walked on.

By the time he reached his street he had stopped noticing he was cold.
