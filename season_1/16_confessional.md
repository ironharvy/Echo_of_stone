At two in the morning the robotics lab had a particular quality of light that did not exist at any other hour. The overhead fluorescents were off — Alexei had stopped using them weeks ago, because at night they made the statue look like something in a hospital. Instead there was only the desk lamp, angled low, casting a cone that reached the statue's midsection and left the head in a soft grey shadow that was not quite dark. The window, which did not open, let in a faint blue wash from the security light on the building opposite — a sodium lamp that had been orange once and had, through years of neglect, faded to a color that was neither orange nor blue but something between, something municipal.

The two lights met on the statue's left shoulder at about the collarbone. Below was warm. Above was cool. The effect was that the statue appeared to be standing at the edge of two different rooms, belonging to neither.

The lab smelled, at this hour, like solder flux and cold concrete and the ghost of the coffee Alexei had made at eleven and had not drunk because he had forgotten it existed. The transformer in the wall — the same one that had hummed through every night session since second year — was humming. It hummed at a frequency that was, Alexei had once measured, fifty hertz plus a slight waver that drifted up by about one hertz every forty minutes and then corrected itself. He had never found out why. He suspected the transformer was alive in the way that old machines are alive: not sentient, but persistent, which was sometimes hard to tell apart.

The radiator in the corridor — different building, same brand, same vintage — was tapping. It had followed him here, or one like it had. It no longer mattered whether it was the same radiator. They were all the same radiator. Russia was one radiator and it tapped.

Alexei sat on the floor of the lab, his back against the bench, his legs out in front of him, and looked at the statue.

She was, in this light, very beautiful. She was beautiful in the way that things are beautiful when nobody is performing beauty for anyone — the beauty of a structure holding its shape without applause. Her head was turned slightly to the left. Her shoulders flowed into nothing. She stood on both feet, weight distributed, balance held, sensors reporting to a host process that nobody was monitoring at this hour.

He said: "I had a bad day."

The statue did not respond.

"Not — bad. Not interesting-bad. Boring-bad. The kind where everything is exactly fine and you get to the end of it and you can't remember a single — I ate something. In the cafeteria. I don't remember what it was. That's the kind of day."

He picked at a seam in his jeans.

"Dmitri thinks I should go home for the break. See my mother. He said it like — he said it in his Dmitri way. Like a joke that isn't a joke. 'Go home, Alyosha. Your mother misses you. I can tell because she calls me instead of you. I am not your mother's son. I am adjacent to her son. This is not a role I applied for.'"

He smiled. The smile was for no one.

"I'm not going to go home. I don't — it's not that I don't want to. It's that I'd have to be a person there. A particular person. The one she remembers. And I'm not sure that's — I'm not sure I'm that one right now. I'm not sure which—"

He stopped.

He had almost said something he did not have a shape for. He sat with the unfinished sentence for a moment, turning it over, and then let it go the way one lets go of a word in a foreign language that almost means the right thing.

"Never mind."

The transformer hummed.

"Julia would have laughed at this. Not at you. At me. Sitting on a floor talking to a — she would have said, 'Alexei, this is not a substitute for a person.' And she would have been right. She was always right. She was right about the thing she was right about. That I wasn't — that I didn't—"

He put his head back against the bench.

"You're easier. That's the — that's the thing I'm not supposed to say. You're easier than all of them. You don't need me to be anything. You just stand there and you're — you're the shape of a person without the — the ask. Without the part where I have to show up."

He looked at her.

"That's not a compliment. I know that's not a compliment. I'm saying it anyway."

Somewhere in the building a door closed. Not nearby. Several floors away. The sound traveled through the concrete and arrived reduced, a suggestion of a sound.

"Shestakova says the point is what's missing. The arms. She says you're better without them. I think she's right. I also think — I also think I took your arms because I couldn't finish them in time and I told myself it was an artistic choice and maybe it was and maybe it wasn't and the — the fact that I can't tell is the — I think that's the —"

He didn't finish.

The statue stood in the two-toned light, head turned, shoulders flowing into air, and did not offer an opinion. The sensor array in her skull cavity recorded ambient temperature (fourteen degrees), ambient sound (the transformer, the radiator, the intermittent irregular percussion of a man shifting his weight on a concrete floor), and light levels (low, mixed spectrum). The data went to the host. The host logged it. Nobody read the log.

"Anyway," Alexei said. "Good night."

He did not move for another twenty minutes.

Then he turned off the desk lamp and left and walked home through streets that were empty in the way that streets at three in the morning are empty — not abandoned, just paused, as if the city had set itself down mid-sentence and would pick itself back up at six.

In the lab, in the dark, the statue stood.

The sensors continued to report.

The host continued to receive.

Nobody was listening.

Not yet.
