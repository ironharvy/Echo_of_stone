The call came on a Sunday morning. Alexei was at his desk, working. Dmitri was on his phone, unshaven, in yesterday's shirt. When he picked up he said *Mama* in the pleasant voice he used for his mother, and then his face did something — did not fall, exactly, but adjusted. Became the face for news that wasn't going to be easy.

He stood. He walked to the window. He said *ah*, and *when*, and *is he*, and after a while he said *I see*, and after that he mostly said *mm* and *I know* and *I know, Mama*, and once *no, she shouldn't, no*, and finally he said *I'll tell him* and hung up.

He stayed at the window a minute longer.

"Valera," he said.

"Your cousin."

"Yeah."

"Is he—"

"He's alive. He — he's in the hospital. Mental ward. He had an — she didn't want to say 'episode' but it was an episode. Second one in six months. They're not letting him out this time."

Alexei did not say anything. There was, he understood, nothing adequate to say.

"He was the funny one in his unit," Dmitri said. "People liked him. He came back five years ago and for five years he was fine. *Mostly* fine. Now it turns out fine had a warranty."

"Dmitri—"

"I'm not — it's fine. I mean. It's not fine. But there's nothing I can do from here. He's not going to want me there. He never — he never wants anyone there."

He turned from the window.

"Also. Senya."

"Senya."

"Senya from our school. Our year. The one with the sister."

"I know Senya."

"He got called up last month."

"Last month?"

"Yeah. His mother told my mother. Apparently he never got into anywhere. Didn't take the exams. Thought he could skip a year. They took him in November. He's gone, I mean — he's serving. Eighteen months. Starting now."

Alexei looked at his notebook, at the schematic of a shoulder joint he had been refining, and the schematic — of a thing attached to a thing, all tolerances satisfied, all forces resolved — looked back at him the way a life raft looks at you, which is to say: not kindly, but available.

"Right," he said.

"That's the news," said Dmitri. "That's the — the Sunday morning news. Isn't it nice. Shall I make tea."

"Yes."

"I am going to make tea. I am going to make tea and I am going to drink it and I am going to then go back to bed, because the — the sun is out and I hate it."

Alexei said: "Dmitri."

"What."

"I'm sorry about Valera."

Dmitri looked at him. It was, Alexei thought later, the longest Dmitri had looked at him in the four years they had known each other. It was not an angry look. It was not a grateful look. It was the look of a man noticing, in passing, that another man was, in fact, present.

"Yeah," Dmitri said. "I know you are."

He went to make tea.

After a while Alexei went back to his work. He worked, that day, for eleven hours. He worked the next day for twelve. He worked past midnight for six consecutive evenings that week, not because the statue required it — it was, by any reasonable measure, finished enough to defend — but because he could not, in his chest, find any other shape for the week's news. The project was the only thing with edges. Everything else was, right now, a room without walls.

He thought, at some point in that week, about the envelope. The envelope his mother had put on the kitchen table on a Thursday, a lifetime ago, that he had never opened and that was still, as far as he knew, in the second drawer of the kitchen sideboard, sitting among the batteries and the warranty cards, waiting with the patient terrible patience of paper.

Deferment lasts as long as I'm enrolled.

So don't fail.

He did not fail.

He worked.

By Friday of the following week he had added a new subroutine to the balance controller, because the subroutine was a thing he could write, and the writing of it was a thing he could do, and the doing of it required his whole attention, which was, right now, what he required of a task.

In the corner of the lab, the statue stood with her two articulated hands lightly at her sides, watching nothing.

Outside, the world continued.

Somewhere in a psychiatric ward in a city Alexei had never been to, Valera was not fine.

Somewhere in a garrison in a town whose name nobody wrote down, Senya was learning what eighteen months meant when they were your eighteen months.

Somewhere on the kitchen table of a house Alexei had grown up in, his mother was setting down a plate too hot to hold, centered exactly.

Alexei tightened a servo by one-eighth of a turn.

It did not help. Nothing helped. That was not, for the purposes of a Friday night alone in a lab, the point.
