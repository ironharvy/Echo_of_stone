The deadline was in three weeks. The defense committee had allocated him a forty-minute slot, which was standard, and a room on the second floor, which was standard, and a rubric that asked him to demonstrate *technical accomplishment, conceptual coherence, and originality*, which was standard and also, in Alexei's case, a problem.

He was trying to finish the hands.

Not the mechanism — the mechanism was done, had been done for weeks, was arguably the best part of the whole build. The hands opened and closed. The fingers articulated independently. The thumb opposed. The grip force was calibrated to hold a glass without crushing it, which was a specification Alexei had set for himself because it seemed like the kind of thing a hand should be able to do.

The problem was the rest of it.

He had been, for the last month, trying to complete the statue's full articulation — shoulders, elbows, wrists, hands — to demonstrate the range of motion the control system could achieve. The defense needed something impressive. Impressive meant complete. Complete meant the full figure, moving, head to fingertip, every joint under control. And the full figure, moving, was drowning him. There were too many degrees of freedom. The balance system fought the gesture system. Every arm position shifted the center of mass and the legs had to compensate and the legs were not, he had to admit, as good as the arms. He had been sleeping in the lab four nights in five — the bench cluttered now with wrappers and cable ties and half-drunk teas gone cold, the corner looking less like a workspace and more like something a person had been living in without admitting it — and the statue still looked, when she moved, like a woman walking through a strong wind she did not believe in.

Shestakova came by on a Tuesday, late afternoon, when the light through the lab windows had gone the color of tea and the building was in its quietest hour — most students gone, the corridor empty, just the hum of equipment and the sound of Alexei not solving the problem.

She did not come to the robotics lab often. She came when she came. She stood in the doorway with her coat still on and looked at the statue the way she looked at student drawings — for a long time, and without needing to speak first.

"You're trying to finish her," she said.

"I'm trying to make her move properly."

"Those are different goals."

He did not reply.

She walked around the statue slowly. She looked at the hands — the beautiful articulated hands that Alexei had spent three hours on the thumb alone — and she looked at the arms, which were good but not as good as the hands, and she looked at the shoulders, which were stiff, and she looked at the whole figure, which was almost a woman and almost moving and almost convincing and not, quite, any of those things.

"You know the Venus de Milo?" she said.

"Of course."

"Why is it famous?"

"It's beautiful. Classical. The proportions—"

"It's missing its arms."

A pause.

"Nobody knows what they were doing. Holding an apple? A shield? We'll never know. And somehow, that makes it more beautiful. You're trying to build the complete version. Maybe you should build the one that survives."

She said this without emphasis. She said it the way she said most important things: as if it were already obvious and she was merely confirming that he had noticed.

Alexei looked at the statue.

He looked at the hands. The hands he had built over three evenings in the lab, five fingers each, every joint measured, the grip force calibrated to hold a glass without breaking it. He looked at the wrists. The elbows. The shoulders that were stiff.

"Cut the arms," Shestakova said.

"I—"

"Not because you can't. Because you choose not to. There is a difference. The first is failure. The second is — the second is the sculpture."

She pulled her coat tighter. She had, he noticed, not taken off her gloves.

"Three weeks is enough time to make the torso and the head perfect. It is not enough time to make everything perfect. You know this. You have known it for a month. I am simply the one saying it to you today."

She left.

Alexei stood in the lab for a long time. The light outside had dimmed to the point where the window had become a mirror, and in it the statue and his own reflection stood at the same depth, one facing him and one not.

Then he disconnected the arms.

It took twenty minutes. He disconnected the shoulder servos, uncabled the control lines, unbolted the structural mounts. He removed the left arm first, then the right. He set them on the bench side by side, palms up, fingers slightly curled.

They looked, laid out like that, like they were waiting for something to be placed in them.

He smoothed the shoulder joints. He sealed the mount points with a clay cap, shaped by hand — by the pads of his fingers, slowly, feeling the contour. The stumps became part of the form. Not a broken place. A finished one. The shoulders flowed into nothing and the nothing was, he could see it now, the point. The eye traveled down from the head to the shoulders and then continued, past where the arms should have been, into the air, and the air completed the thought.

She looked, without her arms, more like herself than she had with them.

He did not know what that meant. He did not examine it. He stood back and looked and breathed and the breath he let out was the first clean breath he had taken in a month.

On the bench, the two detached arms lay palm-up and did not comment.

He covered them with a cloth.

He went home. The night air was cool and tasted of rain that had already happened somewhere else. He breathed it in and the breathing felt new, as if his lungs had been holding something they had only now agreed to let go of.

He slept well that night. The best he had slept in weeks.

The statue stood in the dark, armless, and was, for the first time, exactly what it was meant to be.
