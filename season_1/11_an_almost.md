Her name was Katya. She had told him her name in the first week of the class, easily, the way a person tells you the time. He had said his own name and then realized he had mumbled, and then repeated it, and then felt the heat of having repeated it rise to his face, and Katya had pretended not to notice, which was the thing he would later, without articulating it, have liked about her most.

They worked adjacent for a whole autumn semester without being friends, exactly. It was a relationship made of passing comments. She would glance at his drawing and say something specific — *the shoulder is good today* — and go back to hers. He would glance at hers and not know what to say, and not say anything, and wish later that he had. By the second semester she had started eating lunch at the same table as him, casually, not every day, not ceremoniously. A person being in a room that he was already in.

She wore a thin silver bracelet on her left wrist. A single red bead. He noticed, without thinking about why he noticed, that it was almost the bracelet he had watched on Julia's wrist for seven years and was not.

In March she asked him if he wanted to see something, and he said yes before he had decided to, and they walked to a small gallery two bus stops away where a woman Katya knew was showing drawings — pencil, mostly, large, ungentle. They walked around the room without talking. Katya stopped in front of one drawing — a face with half the features erased — and stood there for a full minute, which was long enough that Alexei felt, obscurely, that the minute was also addressed to him. He did not look at her. He looked at the drawing. He could feel her in his peripheral vision the way one feels weather.

Afterward they walked along the embankment. It was cold and the light was going that specific bluish color the Moscow River gets in late winter when it is pretending to be ice without yet committing. She walked with her hands in her pockets, and he walked with his, and there was a length of silence that was not awkward, which for Alexei was almost a new weather system.

"You don't talk much," she said, not as a complaint.

"No."

"I don't mind. I wanted to say I don't mind."

"Okay."

"You listen, though."

"I think so."

"You do. You listen like it costs you something. Most people listen like they're waiting."

He glanced at her. In the late light her face was — the thought arrived uninvited — *a face you'd want to draw from memory and get wrong on purpose.*

"Do you want to come up?" she said. "I live two streets from here. I have tea. I have — I have tea and toast. I have nothing, essentially, but it's warm."

He wanted to say yes.

He wanted to say yes *very much* — the wanting was so specific it felt rehearsed, which frightened him a little, because he didn't remember rehearsing it.

He said: "I can't. I have to — I said I'd — I told Dmitri I'd—"

It was not true. Dmitri was in Moscow for the weekend at his mother's. Alexei knew it was not true as he said it. He watched himself say it, felt himself step back one pace from his own mouth, and by the time the sentence had finished, he was already in the cold and the apology and the arranging of his face into something that was not what it had been forty seconds earlier.

Katya looked at him.

She looked at him for — not a long time, but long enough that he understood she had seen the shift. She had seen his face be warm and then not be, and she had registered the change without being able to put a name to it, and she had decided — he could watch her decide — that whatever had happened was not about her and was not something she was going to force.

"Okay," she said. Lightly. Kindly. Without insistence. "Another time."

"Yes."

"Actually, Alexei—"

"Yes."

"Never mind."

"What."

She smiled at him. It was a small, sad, practical smile — the smile of a person who had been patient before and who would be patient again and who was, also, aware that patience had a shape and sometimes ran out of it.

"It's fine," she said. "Go find Dmitri."

He turned and walked toward the bus stop.

Ten steps along, he half-looked back, and she was still standing there, watching the river. Her bracelet caught a thin slip of light as she moved her hand in her pocket.

*I always thought you weren't interested.*

The sentence arrived in his head whole, in Julia's voice, at a volume he had not asked for.

He kept walking.

On the bus home he sat by the window with his face against the cold glass and did not look at the other passengers, and he thought, in the quiet mechanical tone of his morning self: *I will call her. On Monday I will call her. I will say I was tired. I will say I would like to try again.*

He did not call her on Monday.

He did not call her on Tuesday.

By Wednesday calling her had become a thing that had, through inaction, acquired weight, and the weight had become an argument for not doing it, and the argument was the argument Alexei always won against himself.

In class on Thursday Katya smiled at him the same as always and asked, briefly, about his drawing, and he was grateful and ashamed in exactly equal measure, and he went on with his day.

The almost closed. It closed cleanly, without injury, the way a door closes when nobody is standing in it.

In the lab corner that evening, working late, he built a hand. Five fingers. Every joint articulated. He spent three hours on the thumb alone. When he was done he set it on the bench next to the half-finished arm and looked at it. It looked, from a certain angle, like a hand on the point of reaching.

He did not attach it. He put it in a drawer. He closed the drawer twice.

He went home.
