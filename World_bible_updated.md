## Core Premise

## Setting & Institutions

**Reality**: Russia-like, present day or near-present
**Sci-fi visibility**: Technology exists but isn't foregrounded until late Season 2; becomes central in Season 3

### Key Locations

**Dubna**: Quiet, technical, unintended consequences. Home of JINR and the university where Alexei studies. Infrastructure is shared, access controls are lax, and things connect that shouldn't.

**Moscow**: Scale, opportunity, emotional contrast. Where Sergei's primary research institute is based.

### Institutions

- Joint Institute for Nuclear Research (JINR)
- High-Energy Physics laboratories
- Dubna University
- University bureaucracy: slow, fragmented, indifferent

### Rule

Institutions are never evil. They are dangerous because they are normal. The catastrophe happens not through malice but through neglectâ€”someone forgot to segment a network, a student had access they shouldn't have, infrastructure built for one purpose got repurposed without review.

## Story Structure

### Season 1: Alexei's POV

We follow what seems like a quiet, minimalist character navigating university, military pressure, and art. The statue project develops. Elena appears briefly during her father's lecture visitâ€”peripheral, incidental. The season ends with the transfer, which from Alexei's perspective registers as something strange happening to his creation, without understanding what.

### Season 2: Elena's POV

We rewind and follow Elenaâ€”her ordinary life, her illness, her relationship with her father, the decision to volunteer for the experiment, and the transfer itself. We see Alexei from outside now. Elena notices his duality, something the audience missed entirely in Season 1. The season ends with her inhabiting the statue, the awkwardness of a new body, and beginning to form connections with a small trusted circle.

### Season 3: The World Expands

The technology becomes known. Corporations want it. Ethics committees debate it. Elena's existence becomes a test case for something larger. Questions of ownership, personhood, and immortality at scale. Both aspects of Alexei and Elena navigate what this means. The karaoke scene likely lives hereâ€”Elena processing her condition through song.

**Tonal goal**: This should feel like "the future we're going to have soon"â€”technology just beyond current capability, with focus on social/economic/ethical consequences rather than mechanisms.

**Possibility (to explore)**: Once Elena's body has been modified enough to read as human at a glance, a "foreign/exchange student" cover could be used to let her move through the world. JINR/Dubna plausibly hosts international students, so an unfamiliar accent, gaps in local knowledge, and no traceable family would read as exchange-student rather than uncanny. Tonally this should be a *pressure* she has to live under (passing, visa/paperwork risk, resentment at hiding what she is) rather than a comfortable disguiseâ€”so the statue-ness stays central and the cover is something she earns, not escapes into.

## Characters

### Alexei Azimov

**Wants**: Postponement, safety, invisibility
**Fear**: Choosing and being wrong
**Core flaw**: Minimizes himself until systems decide for him
**Social pattern**: Quiet presence mistaken for absence
**Military pressure**: Real, looming, non-abstract
**Love**: Early rejection (Julia Knyazeva) â†’ learned emotional minimalism

#### The Duality (UNDER DEVELOPMENT)

Alexei has two selvesâ€”a split that is unknown even to the reader/viewer until Elena inhabits the statue and observes it from outside.

**Self One**: The nerd. Socially awkward, computer science focus, builds the robotics and technical systems.

**Self Two**: The artist. Open-minded, socially capable, shapes the aesthetic form. Creates the Venus de Milo-inspired sculpture.

The two selves are unaware of each other but cooperateâ€”they work on the same project, the statue-robot, each contributing their domain. Neither knows the other exists.

**Thematic purpose**: This is an "over the top" exploration of how humans behave differently depending on environment and people around us. We are all multiple selves; Alexei literalizes this.

**Open question**: Whether this is literal dissociative identity or two separate people (brothers? collaborators?) so enmeshed that Elena initially perceives them as one. The latter is easier to execute; the former is more thematically resonant but harder to justify.

**Narrative function**: Elena is the one who notices and names the duality. This recontextualizes everything the audience saw in Season 1.

### Elena Volkova

**Before illness**: Ordinary, opinionated, alive. Defined as a person before she becomes a patient.
**Core conflict**: Agency vs. survival
**POV**: Minimal presence in Season 1; sharp, emotional, intimate POV in Season 2

- Provides physical/emotional texture for Season 2

#### The Transfer

Elena volunteers for her father's continuity research experiment. She consents because the alternative is deathâ€”this is agency under constraint, not free choice. The transfer succeeds but routes to the wrong host: Alexei's statue-robot, which is connected to JINR infrastructure through negligence and shared networks.

Her father initially believes the experiment failed. Later, he (or someone on his team) realizes it succeededâ€”just into an unintended substrate.

#### The Embodiment

Elena wakes in a body that is:
- Not hers
- Beautiful but incomplete (Venus de Miloâ€”famous for what it lacks)
- Initially without handsâ€”unable to cover herself, gesture, defend
- Naked in front of Alexei (awkward, comedic, grounding the horror in something human)

**What she loses**: Her biological body, her privacy, her assumed future, the ability to pass as normal

**What she gains**:
- Robotic capabilities
- Processing power
- Infrastructure connection (internet in her head)
- Effective immortality
- Physical strength beyond any human

**Tonal note**: If this becomes anime, there should be awkwardness and silliness in the embodimentâ€”comedy that doesn't undermine the tragedy but sits alongside it.

**Open questions**:
- Can she feel? In what way?
- Does she experience time differently?
- Does she need to sleep? Eat? Maintain herself?
- What does she *want* for herself beyond survival?

### Dmitri Semochkin (Alexei's Roommate)

**Role**: Mirror, grounding presence
**Profile**: Same age as Alexei, Computer Science. Loud where Alexei is quiet. Cynical but not cruel. Knows how to "get by."

### Julia Knyazeva (Alexei's Schoolmate)

**Role**: Formative romantic failure
**Profile**: Knows Alexei well enough to matter. Not dramatic, not cruel. Probably kind, socially functional.

## Rules for This Story

1. No character is a caricature
2. No one exists "for the plot"
3. Every secondary character offers a different survival strategy and shows a different failure mode
4. No one in this story thinks they are the villain
5. Institutions are dangerous because they are normal, not because they are evil
6. The catastrophe is an accident that was always going to happen eventually
