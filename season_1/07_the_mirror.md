He came back to the dorm at almost eleven from a session in the robotics lab that had gone badly in a quiet, un-dramatic way. A servo had refused him for an hour in a manner that was, technically, within spec and, practically, mocking. He had fixed it. The fix had been inelegant. He did not feel like he had won anything.

The campus at eleven was a different campus — emptied out, the paths lit by lamps that cast more shadow than light, the buildings reduced to shapes that hummed. The air smelled like wet leaves and the river.

The dorm corridor was warm in the way corridors are warm at eleven — not from heat, but from a day's worth of breath not yet dispersed.

Dmitri was on his bed with a textbook open across his chest, not reading it, using it as a roof. He looked up when Alexei came in.

"You," he said, "look like you lost."

"I didn't lose."

"You look adjacent to having lost."

"I'm going to wash up."

"Wash up *angrily*. I'll wait."

Alexei went into the small shared bathroom at the end of the hall. Two sinks, one mirror, a flickering fluorescent tube that had been flickering since September and would, barring intervention, flicker until June. The tiles were the color of nothing — not white, not cream, just the color tiles become when no one has chosen them. He turned the cold tap. The water came out colder than he expected, cold enough that it felt like information. He splashed his face. Once. Twice. On the third time he made the mistake of lifting his head too fast and catching his own reflection before he was ready for it.

He met his own eyes.

And then — he did not know, afterward, how to describe the and-then. The fluorescent tube clicked. Water ran. His face in the mirror was his face. It continued to be his face. But his face was doing something his face didn't usually do. It was looking at him as if from slightly further back than usual. As if somebody standing just behind him was also looking, through him.

He held still. There was nobody behind him. The tiled wall was empty and slightly yellow.

His mouth opened. Very slightly. He thought he was going to say *hello*, like a fool. He did not say *hello*. He said — soundlessly, mouth only — something else, a syllable he did not catch, the way you sometimes say a name in a dream and wake without the name.

The fluorescent tube clicked again.

He exhaled.

He watched himself exhale.

He felt, for a moment that was shorter than a moment, very slightly amused. It was not an amusement that belonged to the kind of day he had just had. It sat on his face like a borrowed coat. He noticed it fit.

He turned off the tap. He dried his hands on the ragged communal towel. He walked back to the room.

Dmitri, still horizontal under the textbook, looked up and then looked again.

"Huh," he said.

"What."

"Nothing." A beat. "You want a drink?"

Alexei, unpacking his jacket across the back of his chair — not hanging it, drape — considered the question longer than was strictly required for a yes or a no. The room light made the shoulders of the jacket look softer than they were.

"Yeah," he said. Quiet. An almost-smile. "Maybe."

Dmitri sat up.

"Look at you," he said. "Human after all."

"I'm human most of the time."

"You're *functional* most of the time. That's different. I'll find the bottle."

He got up and went rooting in his wardrobe. The middle drawer of the wardrobe, Alexei noticed without quite looking, was hanging slightly open. It was hanging slightly open by about two centimeters. The drawer was in the corner of his field of view and he was, at this moment, aware of it, and he did not close it. He stood by his desk and did not close it. He watched Dmitri rummage, and he picked up the pencil that lay across his own notebook — picked it up not between thumb and forefinger but resting across the pads of three fingers, loose, the way Shestakova had said. He turned it. His thumb traced the small metal rim where the eraser met the wood, once, slowly, as if checking that the rim was still there.

Dmitri came back holding a half-bottle of something brown. He squinted at Alexei across the room.

"Seriously," he said. "What happened to you tonight."

"Nothing happened."

"You look like —" He tried to find the word. He didn't find it. "— like you came back from somewhere."

"I came back from the lab."

"No. I mean — never mind." He uncapped the bottle. "Don't get weird about it. It's nice. It's just also weird."

Alexei almost laughed. It was a small sound and it left his chest before his brain had decided to release it.

"Then don't get weird about it," he said.

Dmitri poured two short glasses, handed one across. The room was quiet now — the guitar student upstairs had given up for the night, and the building had settled into its own low frequencies, pipes and radiators doing their slow administrative work. Alexei took the glass — again, not gripping; resting it on his fingers, feeling the cool of it through the pads. He lifted it, sipped, held the sip, did not grimace.

Dmitri watched him do this. Dmitri did not say anything more about it. Later, lying in bed with the lights out, he would say, vaguely to the ceiling, *weird night*, and Alexei would make a sound of agreement, and neither would elaborate.

At some point in the night — neither of them heard it — the middle drawer of Dmitri's wardrobe, which for reasons no one had articulated had remained slightly open, slid one further centimeter outward on its runner. It stayed there until morning.

In the morning — grey light through the curtain, someone's alarm going off two rooms away and being slapped into silence — Alexei washed his face again in the same flickering light, met his own eyes again and found them ordinary, his own, tired. He walked back into the room, crossed without a word to the wardrobe, and closed the drawer with both hands, pressing it shut until the last half-centimeter seated. He did not remember noticing it the night before.

He went to make tea.
