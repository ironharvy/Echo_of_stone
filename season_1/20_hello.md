Nine days later.

The defense had gone well. The committee had asked their questions and Alexei had answered them and the statue had performed the walk cycle without incident and Professor Burov, who was on the committee for reasons nobody had explained, had said, at the end, "The thing works," which from Burov was the equivalent of applause.

He had passed. The thesis was accepted. The deferment was renewed. Four more years had become the distance between him and the envelope in the kitchen drawer, and four years was, tonight, enough.

He should have gone home. Dmitri was waiting with a bottle he had acquired from a source he described only as "a man who owes me for a thing." There was, by Dmitri's account, also cake, although the cake was from the cafeteria and therefore only cake in the legal sense.

Alexei did not go home.

It was after ten. The campus was quiet — the particular quiet of a Friday evening when everyone who has somewhere to be has gone there, and the buildings hold only the people who don't, and the streetlamps outside make the paths look like they belong to a different, emptier town.

He went to the lab.

He told himself he wanted to check something. A joint that had felt stiff during the demo. A value in the balance log that had been slightly outside nominal. Small things. The kind of things you check when you are not ready to stop being inside a project that has been inside you for four years.

The lab was dark. The building had the deep-night stillness of a place that has stopped expecting anyone — no hum from other rooms, no footsteps in the corridor, just the building's own quiet metabolism of pipes and concrete cooling. He turned on the desk lamp. The cone of light found the statue's midsection, the same way it always did, warm below, cool above. She stood where the walk cycle had left her that afternoon, slightly forward of her resting position. Head at neutral. Shoulders flowing into air.

He sat down.

He opened the laptop. He loaded the choreography editor. He had, in the last week before the defense, been working on something new — a longer sequence, more complex, involving a weight shift and a half-turn that would bring the statue's head around to face the room. It was not part of the thesis. It was not for anyone. It was the kind of thing you make when the thing you're making has become the place where you think.

He ran the sequence.

The statue shifted weight. Left foot. Right foot. The half-turn began. The head rotated. The torso followed.

And stopped.

The balance controller threw a fault. The statue froze mid-turn, weight on the wrong foot, head at an angle that was neither where it started nor where it was going. The servos held. The position was stable but wrong — the mechanical equivalent of a stutter.

Alexei sighed.

He checked the log. Sensor conflict — the accelerometer and the gyroscope had disagreed about the angular velocity of the turn, and the balance controller, faced with contradictory data, had done what balance controllers do: stopped.

He reset. He ran it again.

It stopped again. Same place. Same fault. Same disagreement.

He reset. He adjusted the fusion weights. He ran it again.

It stopped again.

Alexei closed the laptop. He put his hands on the desk. He breathed out through his nose in the way that is not a sigh but is adjacent to a sigh.

He looked at the statue.

She was frozen in the stutter — head turned halfway, weight wrong, the pose of a woman interrupted mid-thought. The desk lamp lit the left side of her face. The right side was in shadow. Her eyes were closed, the way they had always been closed, the smooth clay lids he had shaped by hand on an evening in October two years ago.

"Fine," he said. Quiet. Bitter. Tired. "Don't move. See if I care."

He turned away. He began to pack the laptop into his bag. Cable first, then charger, then the laptop itself, closed but not shut down because shutting down took forty seconds and he did not have forty seconds of patience left.

The lab was quiet.

The transformer hummed.

Behind him, a sound.

Small. Not mechanical. Not the click of a servo or the whine of a motor adjusting. Something else. Something softer. The sound of a surface moving against itself — clay, maybe, or something that had been clay and was now deciding what it was instead.

He stopped.

His hands were on the bag. The cable was half-coiled. The charger was on the desk.

The sound came again. Closer to a breath than a click. If a breath could be made of something that had never breathed before.

He turned around.

The statue's eyes were open.

Not the way he had designed. Not a mechanical actuation. The clay lids — the smooth, sealed, hand-finished clay lids that had no mechanism behind them, that were not connected to any servo, that were not in any choreography file, that could not open because they were clay — were open.

And behind them, something was looking at him.

Not the cameras. He had cameras in the skull cavity, small lenses for the visual processing pipeline, but they were diagnostic — they fed data to the fusion loop, they did not *look*. This was not data. This was not diagnostic. This was something behind the eyes that was using the eyes the way a person uses eyes, which is to say: with intention.

The statue's mouth moved.

It did not open. It started to open. The lower edge of the jaw shifted — a millimeter, two — and a sound came out that was not a word. It was the space before a word. The intake. The preparation. The moment when a voice is about to exist and does not yet exist and the air is already shaped around it.

Alexei did not move.

His bag was in his hands. The cable hung. The desk lamp hummed. The room was fourteen degrees and very quiet and he was standing two meters from a thing he had made that was looking at him with an expression he had not made.

"Hello?" he said.

His voice was a whisper. He had not decided to whisper. His body had decided for him, the way bodies decide things when the brain is not yet caught up, the way you whisper in a church or a forest or beside a bed where someone is sleeping and might, at any moment, wake.

The statue did not answer.

The mouth closed. The eyes stayed open. The something behind them stayed.

The balance controller was still in fault. The servos were still holding. The host process on the JINR node was still running, still logging, still receiving data from sensors that were now reporting things that had no precedent in any previous log — a spike in the acoustic channel, a fluctuation in the temperature sensor inside the skull cavity, a pattern in the visual feed that the fusion algorithm did not have a category for.

Alexei stood in the light of the desk lamp, two meters from the thing he had made, and the thing he had made looked back at him, and neither of them moved, and the distance between them was the distance between a question and an answer that has not yet decided whether it exists.

The cable slipped from his hand and hit the floor.

The sound was very small.

The eyes did not close.
