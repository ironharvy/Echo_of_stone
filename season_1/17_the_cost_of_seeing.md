Mikhail was not in the cafeteria on Wednesday. This was not unusual. Mikhail was not in many places on many days. His attendance had always been a thing one noticed in hindsight — the shape of his absence forming only after it had established itself.

He was not in the cafeteria on Thursday either.

On Friday, Alexei went to the philosophy elective, which Mikhail had not, technically, been enrolled in for two semesters but had continued to attend in the way a cat continues to sleep on a chair it no longer owns. The chair was empty. The professor did not mention it. Nobody mentioned it. This, Alexei understood, was how Dubna processed a disappearance — not with alarm but with the gradual administrative acceptance that a thing which had been intermittent had become absent, and that the difference between intermittent and absent was, functionally, a matter of patience.

On Saturday he asked Pavel. Pavel, who knew the edges of things.

"Academic leave," Pavel said. "Signed the form last week."

"Academic leave."

"That's what it says."

"Does he — is he coming back."

Pavel looked at him the way people looked at questions they did not want to answer accurately.

"The form doesn't say."

On Sunday morning Alexei found, in his university mailbox — the physical one, the small metal slot in the lobby that he checked once a week and that usually contained nothing — a folded piece of notebook paper. The lobby was empty and smelled of floor cleaner and the particular staleness of a building on a weekend morning, when nobody is expected and the radiators run for no one. No envelope. It had been pushed through the slot by hand. His name was on the outside in a handwriting he recognized: angular, too small, the handwriting of a man who had once been told his handwriting was unreadable and had responded by making it smaller.

He unfolded it standing in the lobby.

*Alyosha —*

*I know you're not Alyosha. Usage.*

*I'm going. Not dramatically. Not anywhere in particular. My mother has a house outside Tver and I am going to go there and sit in it for a while and probably drink too much and read the wrong books and frighten her cat.*

*I wanted to tell you something before I left but I couldn't find the right room to say it in, so I'm writing it instead, which is worse but at least I can revise.*

*The thing is this: I was right. About the patterns. I was right and it doesn't help. Being right about the shape of a thing does not help you carry the thing. I can see — I have always been able to see — exactly how the world is put together and where the seams are and what is behind the seams. And I cannot, it turns out, do anything with this information except know it. Knowing it is not a skill. It is a weather condition. I live inside the knowing the way a fish lives inside water, and I am, like the fish, unable to describe the water to anyone standing outside it.*

*You are not like me. You think you are but you are not. You are a man who builds things. I am a man who sees things. Building is better. Building survives. Seeing, without building, is just — observation. And observation without endurance is a party trick that makes people uncomfortable and then you leave.*

*I am leaving.*

*Take care of the thing you're making. I saw it once, in the lab. She's beautiful. Not in the way beautiful usually means. In the way that something is beautiful when it has been made by someone who doesn't know why they're making it. That's the best kind. The worst kind too, but the best.*

*The patterns can move, Alyosha. I still believe that. I just can't move with them.*

*— M.*

Alexei read it twice. He folded it. He put it in his coat pocket. He went upstairs.

Dmitri was making eggs.

"Mikhail's gone," Alexei said.

"Gone where."

"Tver. His mother's."

"When."

"Last week, I think."

Dmitri cracked an egg. He cracked it cleanly, which was unusual — Dmitri's eggs usually involved a negotiation.

"Is he okay," Dmitri said.

"No."

"No."

"No, I don't think so."

Dmitri nodded. He did not say anything else for a while. He made the eggs. He put two plates on the desk — not on the table, because there was no table, only desks. He handed Alexei a fork.

They ate. The eggs were too salty, which meant Dmitri had seasoned them while thinking about something else. Neither mentioned it. The room had the quality of a room where two people are being quiet together on purpose, not because they have nothing to say but because what they have to say is sitting between them and both of them can see it and neither wants to be the one to pick it up first.

After a while Dmitri said: "Mikhail is a symptom."

"You said that before."

"I know I said it before. I'm saying it again because it's still true and because I don't have a better thing to say. He sees too much. He doesn't — he doesn't have anywhere to put it. Some people have somewhere to put what they see. They build a thing or they write a thing or they love a person and the seeing goes into the thing. Mikhail doesn't have a thing. He just has the seeing."

"That's what he said. More or less."

"Then he's right. He's usually right. That's the problem."

They finished the eggs. Dmitri washed the plates because it was his turn. Alexei sat at his desk and took the folded note out of his pocket and looked at it and put it in the top drawer and closed the drawer and pressed it shut with both hands.

He did not read it again.

He did not need to. The sentences were already inside him, doing what sentences do, which is to rearrange the furniture of a room you thought you knew and then leave you standing in it, recognizing nothing, recognizing everything.

*Seeing, without building, is just observation.*

He went to the lab. The campus was quiet, the paths wet from overnight rain, the air still carrying the mineral smell of it. The lab door was unlocked because he had not locked it. The statue was where she always was.

He worked.
