The work took a year. Not in the sense that it consumed the year; in the sense that the year, without quite being asked, made room for it.

They gave him a corner of the robotics lab — a corner, not a bay, because he was a second-year and corners were what second-years got. The corner had a bench, a wall outlet, a window that did not open, and enough floor space for a figure the size of a small woman if the small woman stood very still and did not mind being stared at by undergraduates. Beyond his corner, the lab hummed with other people's projects — the click of keyboards, the whine of test motors, the occasional swear in at least two languages — but the corner itself was his, and he kept it the way he kept things: squared, closed, accounted for.

The first month, the work was all armature.

He welded the spine himself — steel rod, a jig he had built for the purpose, joints reinforced to tolerance, every angle measured twice. He labeled the welds with a silver paint-pen in a tiny hand. He wrote the torque values on masking tape stuck to the bench and did not peel them off when they stopped being current because he liked to see the progression.

The bench, in those weeks, was the bench of a person who took things apart and put them back together, and only that person.

Then sometime in October the work became something else in the evenings.

In the evenings — and this was not a thing Alexei noticed he was doing, because one of the two men doing it did not know the other existed — the armature acquired a shape.

Clay.

He had bought clay from Grisha, who had it because of course Grisha had it. Gray industrial modeling clay, in kilo bars, cheap. It had arrived in a cardboard box that had smelled faintly of fish, for reasons neither of them wished to investigate.

In the evenings Alexei — working loose, hand across the armature, the palm rather than the fingertips — built up the outer shape of a body over the steel. He did not measure. He did not sketch first. He worked from something in his chest that did not translate cleanly into his hands but translated imperfectly, which, Shestakova would have said, was the point.

Morning Alexei did not like what evening Alexei had done.

Morning Alexei would arrive, look at the previous night's progress, and frown. The shoulder was asymmetric. The hip was — he would measure — two degrees off true. He would consider correcting it. He would find himself, for reasons he could not quite articulate, not correcting it. He would instead align the internal servos to the asymmetry evening Alexei had built, tuning the control loop to the shape that existed rather than to the shape that should have existed.

This, he told himself, was engineering to the actual geometry. It was a sensible compromise. It was, technically, the harder problem, because the tolerances had to be derived rather than specified. He was doing it because it was interesting.

He was satisfied with this explanation. He did not examine it further.

Dmitri came by on Sundays with food — always around two, always with something wrapped in paper from the canteen that he had smuggled out with the quiet pride of a man conducting a small important theft. He stood in the doorway and ate a sandwich and made observations.

"Nice statue."

"It's a robot."

"Sure." Dmitri took a bite. "Your robot looks different every time I see it."

"It's a work in progress."

"I know what work in progress means. I'm a student. I'm saying it looks like two people are working on it."

"We're not."

"I'm saying it *looks* like it. It doesn't match itself. Here—" he pointed with the sandwich "—that joint is beautiful, that's like a watch, and then the whole arm above it is — is art school. That's two handwritings."

"It's one person with two moods."

"It's one person with two *handwritings*. I've seen your handwriting. Your handwriting is the joint. Somebody else did the arm."

Alexei did not answer.

Dmitri took another bite of his sandwich and let it go.

In the lab corner, at night, when Alexei was alone except for the faint hum of a transformer in the wall, the statue stood half-made, and the seam where the welded steel met the shaped clay was visible even at three meters. Two handwritings. A figure half-engineered, half-sculpted, belonging fully to neither.

In the dorm, on the nights when the evenings had gone long, Dmitri would wake sometimes to find the middle drawer of his wardrobe hanging open by two centimeters. He would not always say anything. Sometimes he would just push it closed himself, half-asleep, and turn over.

Sometimes the drawer was closed when he woke up.

He didn't always remember whether he had closed it or not.

Through the window that did not open, the seasons changed without his permission — snow piled on the sill in December, melted to a grey film in March, and by April a single weed had pushed through a crack in the outer concrete and was leaning toward the glass as if trying to see what he was building. By February the statue had limbs. By April it had proportions. By the end of the spring semester, the clay was hardened, the armature sealed, the control system provisionally wired, and Alexei — on a Thursday, alone in the lab at eleven, standing back — saw for the first time the shape of the thing without the thing in his way.

It was almost a woman.

She was almost standing.

He thought, without knowing why he thought it, *you look like you have somewhere to be.*

Then he corrected the posture of the left hip by two degrees, sensibly, and went home.
