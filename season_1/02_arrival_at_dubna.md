Dubna at the end of August had the look of a place built for a purpose no one had entirely explained to it. The streets were too wide for their traffic. The trees were tall and confident and uniformly the same age, planted some decade by someone with a plan that had presumably worked. Between the apartment blocks you could see a long stretch of river and beyond it the low pale roofs of the institute, and between the institute and everything else a great deal of sky.

Alexei got off the train with two bags and a folder. The folder contained every document the university had asked for, plus three they had not asked for but that his mother had included *in case*. He had already, on the platform, opened the folder twice to make sure the documents had not evaporated.

The dorm was a concrete building the color of concrete. A woman behind a window who was not listed anywhere in his paperwork took his paperwork, wrote something on a different piece of paper, and handed him a key.

"Room 314."

"Is that—"

"Third floor."

"Thank you."

"It's broken."

"The room?"

"The elevator."

He climbed.

Room 314 had one window, two beds, two desks, two wardrobes, and a smell that was mostly paint and faintly cabbage. The floor had been mopped recently enough that in the corner a small island of damp still held. On the windowsill, facing out, sat a porcelain cosmonaut the size of a hand. Sun-faded to the color of weak tea on one side, still white on the other. A hairline crack ran up the visor. Nobody in the current room list was responsible for it. It had the air of something that had survived several tenants by virtue of not being worth the effort to throw out.

Dmitri's bag was already on the left bed. Dmitri was not. A note was pinned to the lamp with a thumbtack: *AT REGISTRATION. DON'T PUT YOUR THINGS ON MY SIDE. ALSO THE COSMONAUT STAYS. IT LIVED HERE FIRST.*

Alexei put his things on the right side. He set his folder on the right desk, squared it to the edge, and then sat on the right bed and did not move for five minutes.

Then he went down to registration.

Registration was held in a corridor. A corridor, specifically, that connected two other corridors and had therefore been designated as neither. Three folding tables had been set against one wall. Behind each table sat a different woman, and in front of each table curled a different queue, and the queues were not, as far as anyone could tell, for different things.

Dmitri was in the third queue, near the front.

"I'm not in the right line," he said, cheerfully, when Alexei joined him. "I figured this out about twenty minutes ago. I'm staying out of solidarity."

"With whom."

"With the concept."

The woman at the table called a name. Someone two people ahead of them stepped forward with a stack of papers and handed them over. The woman examined the stack. She pulled one sheet from the middle, held it up, and said: "This is not stapled."

The student, who had clearly heard worse things in his life but perhaps not recently, said: "I — there's a staple. On the top."

"This sheet is not attached to the staple."

"It was."

"It is not now."

"I can—"

"You will need to re-submit. After stapling. Next."

The student did not argue. Nobody argued. Arguing was a shape of energy the room had politely declined to accept. The student went to the back of his line with the quietly noble expression of a man preparing to find another staple in a building that very possibly contained none.

"Beautiful," Dmitri whispered. "Like ballet."

When their turn came, the woman examined Alexei's folder with the care of someone defusing a mild but not entirely harmless device. She pulled out three forms. She put one back. She asked for a form he did not have. He produced it from the correct pocket. Her expression did not change, which Alexei understood to be the highest grade she gave.

"Stamp office is in B-building. Deferment paperwork is a separate queue. You'll want the blue form, not the green. The green is for foreign students."

"I—"

"Don't ask why. It has always been the green for foreigners."

"I'm not—"

"I am telling you what it is, not why."

She stamped something. She stamped it again, in a slightly different place, because the first stamp had been insufficiently convincing. She handed him back a thinner folder than the one he had given her, which was, he gathered, a kind of victory.

In the cafeteria that evening they ate something the menu insisted was beef. It was possibly beef. It was certainly a color.

"This is fine," said Dmitri, around a mouthful. "This is honestly fine. My grandfather ate worse."

"Your grandfather was in the siege."

"And he ate worse. The bar is on the floor. We're above the bar. Look at us. Thriving."

Alexei ate. He ate more than he thought he would, in fact, because once he had started the act of eating his body had made a number of decisions without his participation.

Back upstairs, Dmitri tossed his jacket across his desk, emptied half his bag in three gestures, kicked his shoes off in two different directions, and opened the middle drawer of his wardrobe to shove a shirt in. The drawer did not quite go all the way back when he pushed it. It hung in its track with about two centimeters of gap.

Dmitri did not notice. Dmitri was already on the phone with his mother explaining the beef.

Alexei — sitting on his own bed, unpacking his folder with the folded slowness of someone cataloguing a small inheritance — glanced at the drawer.

Then he did not look away.

Then, without deciding to, he stood, crossed the room, and closed the drawer. Carefully. Two-handed. He felt the click of the last half-centimeter find its seat.

He stood a beat longer than he needed to with his palm flat against the drawer face.

He went back to his bed.

Dmitri was still on the phone. "—yes, Mama. Yes, he arrived. He's unpacking. He's fine. He's a nice boy, you've met him. *Mama*. I'm not going to — okay. Okay. Yes. I'll tell him."

He hung up.

"My mother says welcome to Dubna. She says please make sure I don't fail out in the first semester."

"Okay."

"She also says if we're going to starve we should at least starve on something with protein in it. I told her the beef had protein. I lied."

Alexei lay back on his bed. The ceiling was low and very white and had one small discoloration in the corner that resembled, if you squinted, a sleeping dog.

Somewhere on the floor above them someone was learning to play the guitar. They were losing.

From the windowsill, the porcelain cosmonaut faced out at the long river and the low pale roofs of the institute and the great deal of sky, and continued not to comment.

Alexei closed his eyes. He had, he reminded himself, a deferment. The word sat in his chest like a small warm stone. He had a room, and a roommate, and a folder that was now correct. He had a bed. He had four years.

Four years was a long time.

Four years was also not.

He slept.
