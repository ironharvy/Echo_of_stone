The defense was in nine days. Alexei ran the test on a Saturday because Saturdays were when the building was empty and the JINR compute was idle and nobody would walk in and ask what he was doing.

He booted the system at seven in the morning. Coffee from the machine in the hallway — not good coffee, but coffee that had the decency to be hot. He connected the laptop, loaded the choreography sequence he had written the week before, and ran the diagnostics.

Everything checked. Servos nominal. Balance loop stable. Sensor fusion running on the remote host at full rate. The statue stood in the corner of the lab, head slightly turned, weight on both feet, and waited.

"Okay," he said, to nobody. "Let's go."

He ran the sequence.

The statue moved.

It was — he had to admit, watching it — good. The head turned smoothly, fifteen degrees left, fifteen degrees right, returning to center. The torso followed with a two-hundred-millisecond delay that gave the motion a naturalistic quality he had not entirely planned for but would certainly take credit for. The hips adjusted. The weight shifted from center to left foot and back. The balance held.

He ran the second sequence. A step. Left foot forward, weight transfer, right foot follows. The step was small — eight centimeters — and cautious, and the statue held the forward position for three seconds before returning to neutral.

It did not fall.

Alexei exhaled.

He ran the third sequence. Head turn, step, hold. Head turn, step, hold. A simple walk cycle, four steps total. The statue crossed forty centimeters of lab floor with the careful deliberation of someone crossing ice, and stopped, and stood, and did not fall.

"Good," he said. "That's — that's good."

He sat down. He watched the telemetry on the laptop. Everything was within tolerance. The balance corrections were small and fast. The sensor fusion was clean. The host process was consuming twelve percent of the node's capacity, which was well under Pavel's forty-percent ceiling.

He should have been satisfied. He was satisfied. He was also, for a reason he could not locate, unsettled.

He looked at the statue.

She was standing in the position the walk cycle had left her — slightly forward of where she had started, head at neutral, weight centered. The desk lamp was on. The window light was grey. She looked like what she was: a well-engineered sculpture that had just demonstrated coordinated locomotion.

But.

He sat in the chair and looked at her and the *but* sat with him, unnamed, the feeling you get when you enter a room you left empty and something in the room has changed and you cannot identify what has changed and you are probably wrong about something having changed and you are also, in the part of your body that is older than your brain, not wrong.

He checked the telemetry again. Nothing unusual. The sensors were reporting ambient conditions — temperature fourteen degrees, light level low, sound level minimal. The acoustic sensor picked up his own breathing and the transformer hum and a faint click from the laptop's fan.

Nothing.

He stood up. He walked to the statue. He stood in front of her, close enough to see the grain of the clay surface and the fine line where the Artist's shaping met the Engineer's weld.

Her eyes were closed. They were always closed — the face had lids but no mechanism to open them. The lids were clay, smoothed by hand, sealed. She had never opened her eyes because she could not open her eyes.

He looked at her face for a long time.

"You're fine," he said. To her. To himself. "You're exactly what you are."

He reached out and touched the shoulder — the left shoulder, where the arm had been, where the clay cap now sealed the mount point into a smooth curve. The clay was cool. It was always cool. It was clay.

He stepped back.

The feeling did not leave. It sat in his stomach like a meal he didn't remember eating. He packed up the laptop. He disconnected the cable. He turned off the desk lamp.

In the dark, with only the window light, the statue was a silhouette. Head, shoulders, torso, legs. A shape. Nothing more.

He left.

On the walk home, in the thin Saturday light, he told himself that the feeling was fatigue. Nine days before a defense. Not enough sleep. The body produces feelings the mind did not order; this was one of them. He had tested the statue and the statue had passed and the test was good and the data was clean and there was nothing in the data that warranted the feeling.

He was right about the data.

He was wrong about the feeling.

But he would not know that for nine more days.
