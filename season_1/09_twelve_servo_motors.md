The supplier's name was Grisha. The supplier's shop did not have a name. It was in a low brick building behind the Dubna bus terminal, in the part of town that the town had stopped maintaining in the eighties and had, since then, merely tolerated.

You got to Grisha by being told about Grisha. You were told about Grisha by a fourth-year named Sasha who had, two years earlier, been told about Grisha by a fifth-year named Petya, who had since graduated and was now, improbably, working for a bank in St. Petersburg. The chain had a length. It was the only kind of chain it was possible to have.

Alexei and Dmitri arrived on a Saturday morning. It was cold and overcast in the specific way Dubna excelled at: not dramatically cold, just cold enough that you noticed you had always been cold and would continue to always be cold.

Grisha was sixty-something, bearded, and wearing a track jacket that said, in aged white letters, DINAMO. He looked up from a cup of tea when they came in, looked at them both, and returned to his tea.

The shop was one room, very deep, lit by fluorescent tubes at only forty percent of capacity — whether from economy or temperament was unclear. Shelves ran the length of it. The front shelves were organized: bins of resistors, neatly labeled; spools of wire; a respectable inventory of consumer electronic components in unopened packaging. The middle shelves were less organized: loose bags of gears, motors, bearings. The back shelves were not organized at all.

The back shelves were what Alexei would remember.

The back shelves held, quite clearly, the leftovers of a decade of student thesis projects. A quadruped robot with no head and only three legs, propped against the wall. A mechanical hand curled into a permanent fist, wires hanging from its wrist like cut tendons. A small wheeled cart with a single articulated arm holding what had once been a coffee cup. A torso with no limbs, its chest panel open, empty. A face plate — just a face plate, porcelain-white, no eyes cut out yet — leaning on a shelf like it was waiting for someone to finish it. The shelves smelled of old grease and ozone and a particular sadness specific to abandoned ambition.

"What happens to them," Alexei said, quietly, before he had even approached the counter.

Grisha looked up from his tea. He followed Alexei's gaze to the back.

"They defended," he said. "Then they moved out. Then the university called me and said, you want these."

"Why."

"Because I said yes once. Now I say yes always. A system." He shrugged. "Parts are still good. Most of them."

Dmitri was already at the front counter with a list. "We need twelve servo motors. Medium torque. And four stepper motors, and the controllers—"

"Twelve?"

"Twelve."

"Twelve servo motors," Grisha said, slowly. "What are you building. A tank?"

"A sculpture," said Alexei.

A pause.

"A sculpture."

"It moves."

A longer pause.

"You're weird, kid," said Grisha. "I like weird."

He set down his tea. He stood. He went to one of the middle shelves and pulled, from behind a column of unlabeled boxes, a single plastic crate.

"Eight of these in here," he said. "Same spec. Better than what you'll buy new. Refurbished from the metro. Moscow metro had a retrofit two years ago, I got a pallet. The other four — I don't have in stock. I know a guy at the polytechnic who's clearing out a lab."

"Can we just order them through the university?" Alexei asked.

Grisha looked at him the way a plumber looks at someone who has asked whether water flows uphill.

"You can," he said. "You fill out the form. The form goes to procurement. Procurement sends it to the budget office. The budget office loses it. You fill out the form again. They find the first one. Now there are two forms and they don't match. Four months later you get a letter saying the vendor changed and the specs are different. You start over. By then you've graduated."

"So—"

"So I'll call Volodya. He'll sell them to you for what he paid, maybe less — he needs the shelf space. Bring cash, though. He doesn't have a card machine."

"How much."

"A hundred each, maybe one-twenty. Student motors. Nobody wants them."

Dmitri leaned toward Alexei. "We are cutting through bureaucracy," he said, with the solemnity of a man narrating his own heist film. "This is practically civil disobedience."

"This is shopping," Grisha said.

They paid for the metro servos and for a box of miscellaneous bearings that Alexei, on instinct, had selected because they were the right size and also because Grisha, watching him choose them, had looked very faintly approving, and approval from Grisha, Alexei intuited, had a currency of its own.

On the way out, Alexei stopped at the back shelves again. The half-finished face plate with no eye cuts. The wheeled cart with the coffee cup.

"Do any of them still work," he asked.

"Some. Nobody comes for them."

"Why do you keep them."

Grisha considered the question. He scratched his beard. He looked, for a moment, not at the robots but past them, at the brick wall behind.

"Because somebody made them," he said. "If I throw them out, nobody made them."

Alexei did not have a reply for that. He nodded. He followed Dmitri out.

Outside, back in the thin grey cold, Dmitri was already in high spirits.

"We are *criminals* now," he announced to the empty street. "Me and a weird kid. We are *acquiring materiel*. Do you feel the weight of it?"

"Not really."

"I feel the weight of it. I feel very Russian."

"You feel like you want lunch."

"I feel like I want lunch *and* the weight of it. These are not mutually exclusive."

On the bus back, Alexei held the slip of paper Grisha had given him in his coat pocket and turned it between his fingers — by the pads, absently, his thumb tracing the corner — and he did not fully realize he was doing this, because the Alexei who was doing it was not currently in the habit of noticing he existed.

They went for lunch. They had soup. The soup was better than the beef had been. The bar, as Dmitri had pointed out before, was on the floor.

By the end of the week they had eight of their twelve servos. By the end of the next week they had the other four, which Alexei had collected in a stairwell in a building without a name from a man who introduced himself only as *Lev* and did not speak more than was necessary.

The hand, still unbuilt, still living on the right-hand page of Alexei's notebook, began — at the edges of his day, in the gaps between other thoughts — to have fingers.
